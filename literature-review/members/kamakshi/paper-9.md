# 📄 Paper 9: MVP Predicts the Pathogenicity of Missense Variants by Deep Learning
*   **Journal / Conference**: Nature Communications (2021)
*   **DOI**: `10.1038/s41467-020-20847-0`
    **Link**: [Nature Biotechnology]( https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-20847-0)
---

## 🔬 Literature Review Table

| Evaluation Field | Researcher Analysis |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Problem Addressed** <br>*(1-2 lines)* | Most missense variants identified in genetic testing remain "variants of uncertain significance," and existing in silico prediction tools (CADD, PolyPhen2, SIFT, etc.) have limited performance with low positive predictive value, especially in non-constrained genes. Prior methods also don't account for gene dosage sensitivity, which affects how harmful a heterozygous variant actually is. |
| **Methodology & Approach** <br>*(2-3 lines)* |  The authors developed MVP, which uses a deep residual neural network (ResNet) to leverage large training datasets and many correlated predictors. Critically, they trained the model separately for genes that are intolerant of loss-of-function variants versus genes that are tolerant of them, accounting for different modes of genetic action. They compiled cancer mutation hotspots and de novo variants from developmental disorders for benchmarking. |
| **Key Results & Findings** <br>*(2-3 lines)* |  MVP achieved better performance prioritizing pathogenic missense variants than previous methods, especially in genes tolerant of loss-of-function variants. Using MVP, the authors estimated that de novo coding variants contribute to 7.8% of isolated congenital heart disease - nearly double the previous estimate. It also outperformed other tools on cancer mutation hotspot data and de novo variants from autism spectrum disorder studies.  |
| **Limitations Identified** <br>*(1-2 lines)* | A key limitation is the unknown but potentially high false-positive rate of curated pathogenic variants used in training, since large databases like HGMD are compiled from publications of uneven quality. Additionally, a single pathogenicity score cannot capture the full complexity of how variants cause disease - many genes act differently in different diseases (e.g., the same gene can be an oncogene via gain-of-function mutations but cause developmental disorders via loss-of-function mutations), and MVP doesn't distinguish between these mechanisms. |
| **Future Scope** <br>*(2-3 lines)* | The authors suggest that larger sample sizes in future studies would allow more precise estimation of de novo mutation contributions and help identify new candidate risk genes, and that explicitly predicting gain-of-function versus loss-of-function effects (as done for ion channels in other studies) would further improve the utility of such prediction methods. They also expect resources like ClinVar to eventually produce better and larger training data to improve future prediction performance. |
| **Project Relevance** <br>*(1-2 lines)* | MVP sits alongside AlphaMissense in the same problem space — interpreting missense variants - but shows an earlier-generation approach using curated databases and hand-engineered features rather than AlphaFold-based structural learning, useful for comparing how variant-interpretation methods have evolved. |
